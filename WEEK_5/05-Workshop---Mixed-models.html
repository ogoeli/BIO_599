Key Points

Fixed vs.random effects

  • Fixed and random effects affect mean and variance of y, respectively.

  • Examples

      Fixed: Nutrient added or not, male or female, upland or lowland, wet versus dry, light versus shade, one age versus another

  Random: genotype, block within a field, individuals with repeated measures, family, parent

  • Mixed-effects models maximize use of info in the data, compared to 2-stage analysis.

Linear mixed-effects models

  • Random intercept model

  • Random intercept and slope model

  • Random effects model

Model selection and validation

  • Step 1: fit linear regression

  • Step 2: fit model with gls (so linear regression model can be compared with mixed-effects models)

  • Step 3: choose variance structure

    • Introduce random effects, and/or
    • Adjust variance structure to take care of heterogeneity

  • Step 4: fit the model

    • Make sure method="REML"

      M1.lme=lme(Form, random=~1|Nest, method="REML", data=Owls)

  • Step 5: compare new mixed-effects model with old

    Form <- formula(LogNeg~SexParent*FoodTreatment+SexParent*ArrivalTime)

    M.gls = gls(Form, data=Owls)

    M1.lme=lme(Form, random=~1|Nest, method="REML", data=Owls)

    anova(M.gls, M1.lme)

  • Stept 6: validate model and, if necessary, repeat steps 4 and 5 until good model is found

  • Steps 7 and 8: find optimal fixed effects structure

    First, interaction terms; drop one at time and compare models

    Use methods="ML"

  • Step 9: refit with REML and validate model

  • Step 10: interpret

Analysis Example

The data:

Data was collected for 3 different fish species (S1-3) with 10 individuals sampled per species across 6 different lakes (L1-6). Individuals from each species were measured and exhibit variation in their body length and trophic position.

#read in the data
fish.data <- read.csv(file="../../Data files/fishdata.csv")

The underlying question for these data is: does fish trophic position increase with fish size? This relationship might differ slightly among species due to unmeasured biological processes (e.g.growth rate) or among lakes due to unmeasured environmental variables. But we are not interested in this variation, so we need to find a way to control these potential effects while still maximizing the use of our data.

Thus, a mixed effects model for nested data is applicable in this case! All analyses are done using the package {nlme} but can also be done in {lme4}

library(nlme)

In linear mixed models, intercepts and/or slopes are allowed to vary according to a given factor (random effect; e.g.by lake and/or species).

Allowing intercepts and/or slopes to vary by random effects means that you assume they come from a normal distribution. A mean and standard deviation of that distribution are estimated based on your data. The most likely intercepts and slopes from that distribution are then fit by optimization (ex. maximum likelihood or restricted maximum likelihood).

Check collinearity

The problem with collinear predictors is simply that they explain the same thing, so their effect on the response variable will be confounded in the model.

In this example, there is no risk of collinearity with only one continuous variable. If you had another continuous variable (var2), one simple way to check for collinearity is cor(var1, var2)

Consider scale

If two variables in the same model have very different scales, the mixed model will likely return a convergence error when trying to compute the parameters. The Z-correction standardizes the variables and solve this problem.

Body scale = long scale Trophic position = short scale

Because our data have very different scales of variation, we apply the Z-correction

# Standardized length, "by hand"
fish.data$Z_Length <- (fish.data$Fish_Length - mean(fish.data$Fish_Length)) / 
                      sd(fish.data$Fish_Length)

# Standardized trophic position, with the function scale
fish.data$Z_TP <- scale(fish.data$Trophic_Pos)

Interpreting scaled variables When a variable is scaled (standardized), a one-unit increase in the scaled version corresponds to a one standard deviation increase in the original variable. This makes model coefficients easier to interpret, especially when units differ dramatically.

Step 3: Choose a Variance Structure

Last week we talked about the optimal variance structure in terms of heterogeneity. We can still do that here, but adding the random component is our first priority. Note that the random intercept is also part of the choose a variance structure process.

We should choose our random structure based on knowledge of the study design but can also look at residual plots of the linear model to assess if there is potential variance associated with our proposed random effects.

Remember we want to determine if the trophic position can be predicted by body length, while taking into account the variation between species and lakes.

These results suggest that there is residual variance that could be explained by these factors, so they should be included in a mixed effect model!

Step 4: Fit the model

We will ignore the effect of Fish_Species here and only use Lake. Note that this will violate the assumption of independence - but given that there are only 3 fish species it should be included as fixed effect.

library(nlme)
M1.lme= lme(Z_TP ~ Z_Length, random=~1|Lake, 
     data = fish.data, method="REML")
A note on estimation methods

ML (Maximum Likelihood) is the default method in lme. REML (Restricted Maximum Likelihood) is the default method in lmer (see ?lmer).

The Maximum Likelihood (ML) method underestimate model variances by a factor of
(nk)/n, where k is the number of fixed effects. The REML method corrects for this bias.

REML estimates can be used when comparing models with the same fixed effects (i.e.nested models). However, if you are comparing models where the fixed effects differ among models then maximum likelihood should be used to fit parameters as this method is not dependent on the coefficients of the fixed effects. Fitting using maximum likelihood is done by setting REML=FALSE in the lmer command.

In summary: * REML to compare models with nested random effects and the same fixed effect structure * ML to compare models with nested fixed effects and the same random effect structure * ML to compare models with and without random effects

Both lme() and lmer() fit linear mixed-effects models, but they differ slightly:

lmer() allows for more flexible random effect structures (e.g., random slopes), but does not directly support correlated residual structures or variance weights.

lme() supports correlation structures and weighted variance, which can be useful in ecological data with heteroscedasticity or temporal autocorrelation.

Manually checking residuals is the gold standard for understanding why we diagnose models the way we do. However, the {performance} package also provides a convenient one-function check that summarizes the most important assumption tests and diagnostics in one place.

library(performance)
check_model(M1.lme)

This produces a set of diagnostic plots and summaries.

Note for this model you will see only three diagnostic panels:

Residuals vs.Fitted (Linearity)

Normal Q-Q Plot (Normality of Residuals)

Histogram/Density Plot of Residuals (Normality of Residuals)

This is expected because some diagnostics are not available for {nlme} model objects or cannot be computed with a single fixed effect.

Why you might see fewer panels in general:

Collinearity checks require more than one fixed effect.

Leverage/influence plots are not available for all {nlme} models.

Singularity checks are only computed for {lme4} models.

How to read the output (all possible panels):

  1. Residuals vs.Fitted
    Checks for homoscedasticity (equal variance).
    Points should be evenly scattered around the horizontal line at 0 with no clear pattern.
    A funnel shape indicates heteroscedasticity; curvature suggests a missing nonlinear term.

  2. Normal Q-Q Plot
    Checks whether residuals are approximately normally distributed.
    Points should follow the diagonal line closely.
    Heavy tails or strong curvature indicate deviations from normality.

  3. Histogram of Residuals
    Another way to assess normality.
    Look for symmetry around zero; strong skewness may need transformation or reconsideration of model structure.

  4. Leverage and Influence
    Identifies observations with high leverage (x-axis) or large influence (y-axis).
    Points outside the shaded area may disproportionately affect model estimates investigate these individually.

  5. Collinearity Check
    If you have multiple fixed effects, this panel checks variance inflation factors (VIF).
    VIF > 5 suggests potential multicollinearity issues.

  6. Singularity Check
    For random effects: warns if one or more variance components are estimated as zero (possible overfitting).
    If flagged, consider simplifying your random effects structure. When fitting complex random effect structures with lmer(), you may see a warning that the model is singular. This means that one or more variance components (e.g., random slopes) are estimated as essentially zero.

You can also check for singularity using this:

isSingular(M1.lmer)
## [1] FALSE

If TRUE, your model may be overfit consider simplifying by removing one of the random effects.

Lets go section by section and try to understand what we are looking at.

The output is broken up into descriptions of the Random effects (things we allow to vary based on the normal distribution) and Fixed effects (things we estimate in the same way as classical regression):

Random effects: Groups: grouping factors

Name: (Intercept) for the intercepts or the name of the variable on which the random slope is estimated (Z_length in this example)

Std.Dev. is the standard deviation of the estimated effect. The variance of the effect is St.Dev^2. We can use this to calculate the Intraclass Correlation Coefficient (ICC). The ICC is calculated as the ratio of variance between the random effect and the total variance. Thus, the ICC describes the proportion of variance in the response variable that is attributed to a specific random effect Variance = St.Dev^2 ICC = (Variance of the Effect)/(Variance of the Effect + Residual Variance)

In this case, ICC = (0.42882)/(0.42882 + 0.8172672^2) = 0.2158611 So the difference between lakes explains ~20% of the varaince that is left over after the variance explained by our fixed effects.

Corr the correlation between the random intercept and the random slope for a given grouping factor.

Fixed effects: This part presents the fixed effect estimates.

These statistics could be used as it is (although be careful of interpreting the p-value). You could also calculate the 95% confidence interval (CI) with this equation:

CI = Estimate +- 1.96 *Std. Error

If 0 is in the interval, then the parameter is not significantly different from zero at a threshold of = 0.05.

Optional - R2 statistics

library(MuMIn)
r.squaredGLMM(M1.lme)
##            R2m       R2c
## [1,] 0.1751494 0.3532371

The r.squaredGLMM() function from the {MuMIn} package provides two types of R-squared values for mixed-effects models:

Marginal R (R2m): the proportion of variance explained by the fixed effects alone.

Conditional R (R2c): the proportion of variance explained by both fixed and random effects combined.

In this case:

R2m = 0.175: About 17.5% of the variation in trophic position is explained by fish length (the fixed effect).

R2c = 0.353: About 35.3% of the variation is explained when we account for both fish length and variation among lakes (the random effect).

This tells us that lake-level differences explain a substantial portion of the remaining variation nearly doubling the explanatory power of the model when included.

Random Intercept and Slope Model

In the main analysis, we accounted for variation among lakes by allowing intercepts to vary (i.e., different mean trophic positions per lake). Now well explore whether the relationship between body length and trophic position (the slope) also differs by lake. This can be done by fitting a random slope model.

Fit the model with a random intercept and random slope

M.randslope <- lme(Z_TP ~ Z_Length,
                   random = ~ Z_Length | Lake,
                   data = fish.data,
                   method = "REML")

Including (Z_Length | Lake) means each lake has its own intercept and its own slope for the Z_LengthZ_TP relationship. You are allowing the slope of the relationship between fish length and trophic position to vary by lake. This is useful when different groups might not only start at different intercepts, but also have different relationships between predictors and the response.

Compare with random intercept-only model

To determine whether the more complex model improves fit, we compare it to the random intercept-only model using a likelihood ratio test.

anova(M1.lme, M.randslope)
##             Model df     AIC      BIC   logLik   Test      L.Ratio p-value
## M1.lme          1  4 462.814 475.5412 -227.407                            
## M.randslope     2  6 466.814 485.9047 -227.407 1 vs 2 6.540267e-08       1

If the model with random slopes has a lower AIC and the LRT p-value is small (e.g., < 0.05), this suggests that lakes differ not only in their average trophic position but also in how body size affects trophic position. That is not the case here. And we would not use this model over the random intercept model.

Appendix: Common mixed model syntax

Below is a reference for common tasks when working with linear mixed-effects models:

Random intercept model
lmer(y ~ x + (1 | group), data = dat)

Random intercept and random slope model
lmer(y ~ x + (x | group), data = dat)

Compare two models
anova(model1, model2)

Extract R values (marginal and conditional)
library(MuMIn) r.squaredGLMM(model)

Check model assumptions visually
check_model(model)

Extract fixed effects
fixef(model)

Extract random effects
ranef(model)

Exercise 3: Telomere Length in Aging Birds

This dataset (birdmalariaTL.csv) includes telomere length data for birds of varying ages, some of which are chronically infected with malaria. Youll test whether infection accelerates aging (via telomere shortening), while accounting for repeated observations within individuals.

  1. Read in the dataset birdmalariaTL.csv.

  2. Fit a random intercept model with:

    • Response: TL (telomere length)
    • Predictors: Sex, year, Age, infected, and Age * infected
    • Random intercept: Ind (individual bird)
      > What does the coefficient for Age:infected tell you about aging in infected birds?

  3. Fit a random intercept and slope model allowing the slope of Age to vary by Ind.
    > Why is this more appropriate when Age varies within individual?

  4. Compare the models in terms of:

    • Coefficient estimates for Age and Age:infected
    • Confidence intervals
      > What changes do you see in the estimates and their uncertainty?

  5. Use AIC or likelihood ratio tests to compare model fit.
    > Which model would you choose and why?

  6. Check model assumptions using check_model().
    > Are residuals and random effects approximately normal? Any signs of heteroscedasticity or outliers?

  7. Predict telomere length for an infected female born in 1991, age 4, using predict() or matrix multiplication.
    > How would you explain this prediction to a non-statistical audience?

Exercise 4: Telomeres and Parental Infection

In this dataset (birdmalaria0.csv), youll model early-life telomere length in offspring birds in relation to maternal age and malaria infection status, accounting for family structure.

  1. Read in the dataset birdmalaria0.csv.

  2. Fit a mixed-effects model with:

    • Response: OffBTL (offspring telomere length at day 9)
    • Fixed effects: Sex, broodyear, mage, mmal, and mage:mmal
    • Random effects: dam (mother ID) and brood (brood ID)

  3. Check model assumptions using check_model().
    > Comment on normality of residuals, residual vs.fitted patterns, and any heteroscedasticity.

  4. Evaluate the significance of the interaction mage:mmal using a likelihood ratio test.
    > What does this interaction mean biologically?

  5. Visualize model predictions using:

    • ggpredict()
    • Or custom ggplot2 visualizations with fitted lines and ribbons
      > What do the plots suggest about the effects of maternal age and infection on offspring telomere length?
